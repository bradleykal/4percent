The Funding Crisis

A data-driven look at why childhood cancer research is severely underfunded—and what it costs us.

By the Numbers

The disparity between pediatric cancer's burden and its research investment is stark and undeniable

4%

Federal Cancer Research Funding

Of all NCI cancer research dollars, only 4% goes toward childhood cancers—despite cancer being the #1 disease killer of children in the U.S.

$195M

Annual Pediatric Cancer Budget

All childhood cancers combined receive approximately $195 million per year, compared to $5 billion for adult cancers—a 25x disparity.

185 vs 3

New Drug Approvals (20 Years)

In the past two decades, 185 new cancer drugs were approved for adults. Only 3 were developed specifically for pediatric cancers.

17,000+

Children Diagnosed Annually

More than 17,000 children under 18 are diagnosed with cancer each year in the United States—that's 46 families every single day.

60%

Survivors with Long-Term Effects

Nearly 60% of childhood cancer survivors experience severe, disabling, or life-threatening health complications as adults due to outdated treatments.

22%

Children in U.S. Population

Children under 18 represent 22% of the U.S. population—yet receive only 4% of cancer research funding. This gap is indefensible.

Why Does This Happen?

The funding gap is the result of multiple systemic failures: adult cancer has larger advocacy groups, more political visibility, and a far larger market for pharmaceutical companies. Childhood cancers are individually rare (though collectively significant), making them commercially unattractive for drug development. Federal funding formulas have not kept pace with the documented need.

The result: children are treated with drugs developed for adults, often at great long-term cost to their health. It's not a research question—it's a policy and priorities problem.
