The Doctor Pay Gap

Pediatric oncologists spend the same years training as their adult counterparts—yet earn dramatically less. This disparity is driving a shortage in childhood cancer care.

Compensation vs. Commitment

The financial penalty for choosing to care for children with cancer is real, significant, and getting worse

$120K

Annual Pay Gap

The average pediatric oncologist earns approximately $120,000 less per year than an adult medical oncologist—for the same level of training and responsibility.

15 Yrs

Training Required

Becoming a pediatric oncologist requires 4 years of medical school, 3 years of pediatrics residency, and 3+ years of oncology fellowship—equal to or longer than adult oncology training.

40%

Lower Reimbursement

Medicare and Medicaid pay significantly less for pediatric oncology procedures than equivalent adult services, creating structural underpayment baked into the system.

After accounting for student debt (average $200,000+), delayed earnings, and opportunity cost, many pediatric oncologists face a lifetime earnings gap of $1–2 million compared to peers in other specialties.

Why This Matters for Kids

The pay gap creates a pipeline problem. Medical students facing $200,000+ in debt are rational actors—when choosing between specialties with equivalent training and prestige, pay matters. Many qualified candidates who would excel at pediatric oncology are steered toward higher-paying adult oncology practices.

The result is a shrinking workforce of pediatric oncology specialists, longer wait times for families, and geographic deserts where children with cancer must travel hundreds of miles for care. Fixing the pay gap isn't just about fairness to doctors—it's about access for kids.
